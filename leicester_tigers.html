    • About The Leicester Tigers

    Established in 1880, the Leicester Tigers stand as one of English rugby's most illustrious institutions. Based at Welford Road Stadium, this powerhouse has earned a formidable reputation, boasting an impressive collection of silverware. With ten Premiership titles and back-to-back European Cup victories in 2001 and 2002, the Tigers have consistently been at the forefront of domestic and international success. The iconic red, green, and white jersey symbolizes their rich history, while legendary players like Martin Johnson and Neil Back have contributed to the club's legacy. The Welford Roar, echoing through the historic stadium, is a testament to the unwavering support from the passionate fan base that has witnessed the Tigers' triumphs and resilience over the years, making Leicester Tigers a true titan in the world of rugby.

    Foundation and Tom Crumbie Era (18801928): Building the Legacy

    Leicester Football Club, born from the merger of three smaller teams in 1880, embarked on a journey marked by innovation and triumph. Under the 33-year secretaryship of Tom Crumbie starting in 1895, the club rose to national prominence by disbanding reserve teams and clinching their first silverware, the Midlands Counties Cup, in 1898. Leicester's status as a premier club solidified with notable fixtures like facing The Original All Blacks in 1905 and hosting the Invincible All Blacks in 1924. The club weathered challenges during the Lions captains' era, witnessing Doug Prentice's leadership and Bernard Gadney's contributions. Despite a challenging period in 1936-37, the club's resilience set the stage for a record attendance of 35,000 against the Invincible All Blacks.

    Lions Captains, Decline, and Club Restructure (19281971): Navigating Challenges

    The era from 1928 to 1971 saw Leicester Tigers navigating highs and lows. Club captain Doug Prentice's leadership during the 1930 British Lions tour and Bernard Gadney's role as the first home-produced England captain in 1934 marked significant milestones. The first BBC radio broadcast of a Tigers game in 1930 and the introduction of regional "Merit Tables" in 1976-77 showcased the evolving landscape. The club's transition to a more traditional membership-based approach in the 1956/57 season and the coaching tenure of Chalkie White in 1968 reflected the club's adaptability. Despite challenges in the late '60s, the club laid the groundwork for future successes.

    Introduction of Competitions (19711978): Pioneering Changes

    The 1971/72 season brought pivotal changes with the introduction of a national Knockout Cup competition and Tigers' first "Youth" XV. Leicester's journey in the Cup, regional "Merit Tables," and reaching the Twickenham final in 1977-78 highlighted their competitive spirit. The expansion of competitions set the stage for increased popularity, with the attendance at the 1977-78 final more than doubling the previous season. This period also witnessed Leicester's membership doubling from 750 in 1978 to 2,000 by the end of 1979, signaling a growing fan base.

    Centenary and Cup and League Success (19791988): Triumphs and Celebrations

    The late '70s and '80s marked a golden period for Leicester Tigers. Securing their first national trophy, the 197879 John Player Cup, set the tone for subsequent successes. Retaining the cup in 197980 and winning the Midlands Merit Table showcased their dominance. The centenary celebrations in 1980, including a tour to Australia and Fiji, reflected the club's stature. Tigers' Dusty Hare breaking the world record for points scored in 1981 and retaining the cup in 198081 underlined individual and team accomplishments.

    Professional and European Success (19932003): A Decade of Dominance

    The transition to professionalism from 1993 to 2002 marked a remarkable era for Leicester Tigers. Their nine trophies in ten years, starting with the 1993 Pilkington Cup, showcased consistent excellence. Under Director of Rugby Dean Richards and subsequent coaches, Tigers secured four consecutive Premiership titles from 1999 to 2002 and back-to-back Heineken Cups in 2001 and 2002. The club's dominance at home, going 57 games unbeaten from 1997 to 2002, solidified their status as a rugby powerhouse.

    Premiership Success (20042013): Resilience and Renewed Triumphs

    The period from 2004 to 2013 saw Leicester Tigers navigating challenges and reclaiming success. Pat Howard, Marcelo Loffreda, and Richard Cockerill's coaching stints showcased the club's adaptability. Premiership titles in 2009 and 2010, coupled with domestic league and cup doubles, highlighted their resilience. Despite European Cup final losses, Tigers' consistent presence in playoffs showcased their competitive edge. The 2013 triumph, winning their 10th English title, marked a record-extending achievement, solidifying their standing in Premiership history.

    Championship Drought (20132020): Transition and Challenges

    The post-2013 era brought challenges for Leicester Tigers. Despite reaching Premiership finals in 2011 and 2012, the club faced setbacks, including a change in coaching staff. The departure of key players, shifts in leadership, and missing playoffs in 2018 signaled a transitional phase. The 2019-20 season, impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, saw financial strains and player departures. Geordan Murphy's departure in 2020 marked the end of an era.

    Borthwick in Charge (20202022): Rebuilding and Triumphs

    With Steve Borthwick taking the helm, Leicester Tigers entered a new phase. The 2020-21 season saw a resurgence, reaching the European Rugby Challenge Cup final and securing Champions Cup rugby. The subsequent season showcased a strong start, breaking records and securing a home play-off semi-final. A dramatic win in the Premiership final against Saracens, securing their 11th English title, marked a remarkable turnaround. Borthwick's leadership brought renewed optimism, and Leicester Tigers emerged as a force to be reckoned with.

    Speculations and Transitions (2022Present): Future Paths

    As speculations surround Steve Borthwick's potential role as England head coach, Leicester Tigers navigate uncertainties. The 2022-23 season showcased promise with a strong start and notable achievements in domestic and European competitions. With coaching transitions and the potential departure of key players, the club faces challenges and opportunities. The rich history and resilience of Leicester Tigers position them as a dynamic force in the ever-evolving landscape of rugby. The future unfolds with anticipation, as the club continues its legacy in the world of rugby.
