    • About Wales Rugby

    Wales embarked on their Test rugby journey in the late 19th century, playing their inaugural match against England in 1881. The Welsh national team has a storied history, reaching its pinnacle in the professional era. Notable moments include their triumph in the inaugural Rugby World Cup in 1987, where they showcased their prowess against the world's best. Captained by Sir Gareth Davies, Wales made a lasting impact, laying the foundation for future successes. The team continued to be a force in international rugby, securing Grand Slam victories in the Six Nations and achieving notable wins against southern hemisphere powerhouses. The charismatic leadership of captains like Sam Warburton and Alun Wyn Jones has defined an era of Welsh rugby marked by resilience and skill. With a rich history and a commitment to excellence, Wales remains a formidable presence on the global rugby stage.

    Early Years: 1881–1892

    Rugby union took root in Wales in 1850, thanks to Reverend Rowland Williams, who introduced the sport at St David's College, Lampeter. The Welsh played their first international match in 1881 against England, marking the beginning of a rich rugby history.

    First 'Golden Age' (1893–1913)

    With the introduction of the "four three-quarters" formation, Wales won the Home Nations Championship in 1893, securing the Triple Crown. This marked the start of a golden era that lasted until 1911, featuring victories in 1900, 1902, and 1905, with the first Grand Slam achieved in 1911.

    The Game of the Century

    In 1905, Wales faced the formidable Original All Blacks, defeating them 3-0 in a historic match at Cardiff Arms Park. This victory remains iconic in rugby history, being the only loss for the All Blacks during their 35-match tour.

    Post-war Years (1920–1968)

    The post-World War I years saw a decline in Welsh rugby, mirroring economic challenges. The 1930s witnessed a resurgence, with Wales winning their first championship in nine years in 1931.

    Second 'Golden Age' (1969–1979)

    The 1970s marked a second golden age for Wales, with world-class players dominating Northern Hemisphere rugby. Notable victories, including a Grand Slam in 1971, showcased Wales' rugby prowess.

    Revival under Ruddock and Coaching Changes (2004–2007)

    Coached by Mike Ruddock, Wales achieved a Grand Slam in 2005, breaking a 27-year drought. However, coaching changes and challenges ensued, leading to Warren Gatland's appointment.

    Gatland Era (2008–2019)

    Under Warren Gatland's leadership, Wales experienced success, claiming Grand Slams in 2008 and 2012. Notable World Cup performances, including reaching the semi-finals in 2011 and 2019, showcased Wales' global competitiveness.

    Pivac Era (2019–2022)

    Wayne Pivac succeeded Gatland in 2019. Despite a challenging start, Wales won the Six Nations in 2021 and reached the top spot in the World Rugby Rankings. However, subsequent performances, including a fifth-place finish in the 2022 Six Nations, led to coaching changes.

    Return of Gatland (2022–Present)

    Warren Gatland's return as head coach in 2022 marked a new chapter for Welsh rugby. With a coaching team in place, including Alex King, Mike Forshaw, Jonathan Humphreys, and Neil Jenkins, Gatland aims to guide Wales through the 2023 Rugby World Cup and potentially beyond.
