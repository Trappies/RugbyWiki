    • About The Rebels

    The Melbourne Rebels, established in 2010, quickly became a formidable force in Australian rugby. As the newest addition to the Super Rugby competition, the team embarked on a mission to carve its place in the rugby landscape. In their debut season, captained by Stirling Mortlock, the Rebels showcased determination despite facing the challenges typical for newcomers. Over the years, the team evolved, witnessing highs and lows, with standout performances from players like Kurtley Beale and Will Genia. The Rebels' commitment to developing Australian rugby talent and their spirited performances in Super Rugby and Super Rugby AU have solidified their presence in the sport, earning them respect and recognition as a powerhouse in the Southern Hemisphere's rugby scene.

    Inception and Ascent: Melbourne's Bid for Super Rugby Franchise

    Amidst the era of professionalism in rugby union, the restructuring of the Super 10 competition post the 1995 World Cup set the stage for the formation of SANZAR, overseeing a 12-team provincial union from Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa. Melbourne's journey began with bids for the 13th and 14th franchise in 2002, showcasing its rugby enthusiasm through successful hosting and public support. Despite setbacks, including the Western Force winning the bid, this marked Melbourne's initial foray into professional rugby.

    Battle for the 15th Super Rugby License

    The desire for expansion led to the creation of an additional Super Rugby license, sparking a competitive bidding process in 2009. Victoria, backed by various consortiums, faced intricate negotiations and arbitration. Despite challenges, including financial considerations and debates over broadcasting rights, Melbourne emerged victorious, securing the 15th Super Rugby license and officially becoming the Melbourne Rebels.

    Establishment and Leadership Dynamics

    The ownership license transitioned to Harold Mitchell's consortium in 2010, initiating the establishment phase of the Melbourne Rebels. Leadership witnessed multiple shifts, from coaching appointments to CEO changes, navigating challenges such as the resignation of Brian Waldron due to salary cap issues. The dynamic evolution of leadership underscored the franchise's commitment to stability and growth.

    Financial Challenges and Strategic Sale in 2015

    The Rebels faced financial burdens, contributing significantly to the Australian Rugby Union's fiscal deficit in 2014. In a strategic move to alleviate financial pressures, the ARU announced the sale of the Rebels to Imperium Sports Management in June 2015. This marked a pivotal moment, signaling a shift in ownership dynamics and financial restructuring for the Melbourne Rebels.

    Survival Amidst Super Rugby Restructuring (2017)

    The threat of a reduced Super Rugby competition in 2017 put the Rebels in a precarious position, with the potential for one Australian franchise to be axed. In a bold move, CEO Andrew Cox sold the franchise back to the Victorian Rugby Union for $1, safeguarding the Rebels from potential elimination. The subsequent removal of the Western Force solidified the Rebels' survival as a Super Rugby side.

    Legacy and Community Integration

    The transfer of shares to the Victorian Rugby Union in 2013 marked a strategic decision to promote rugby's growth in Victoria, aligning professional and community objectives. The combined operations under new leadership, including CEO Rob Clarke, emphasized a collaborative approach. This integration highlighted the Rebels' commitment to fostering rugby at both grassroots and professional levels.
