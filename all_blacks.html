    • About The All Blacks

    The All Blacks, New Zealand's national rugby union team, stand as one of the most formidable and successful teams in the history of the sport. Renowned for their iconic silver fern emblem and traditional black jerseys, the team has an illustrious legacy dating back to their first international match in 1903. The All Blacks have consistently dominated world rugby, claiming numerous Tri-Nations and Rugby Championship titles. Their intensity, skill, and the haka, a powerful Maori war dance performed before matches, make them a symbol of strength and unity. With an exceptional win record and back-to-back Rugby World Cup victories in 2011 and 2015, the All Blacks continue to embody excellence in rugby and command respect on the global stage.

    Introduction of Rugby to New Zealand

    Rugby, known universally in New Zealand as just "rugby," was introduced to the nation in 1870 by Charles Monro. The first recorded game took place in Nelson in May 1870, and the Canterbury Rugby Football Union, New Zealand's first provincial union, was formed in 1879. The first official New Zealand side toured New South Wales in 1893, captained by Thomas Ellison, winning nine out of ten matches. The team's first true test match occurred in 1903 against Australia, resulting in a 22–3 victory at the Sydney Cricket Ground in front of over 30,000 spectators.

    The Original All Blacks (1905–06)

    The representative New Zealand team, known as the "Originals," toured the British Isles in 1905. The team played 35 matches, winning 34, with their only loss being a controversial 0–3 defeat to Wales in Cardiff. Despite on-field success, the team faced criticism for not adhering to the amateur and gentlemanly spirit promoted by the International Rugby Football Board. The success of the Originals led to the formation of a professional team, the "All Golds," in 1907, which toured the British Isles and played rugby league.

    Development of a Legacy

    International rugby was suspended during World War I, but a New Zealand Services team competed in the inter-services King's Cup. The 1924 All Black tourists to the British Isles and France, known as "the Invincibles," won every game but were deprived of a potential grand slam. In 1937, South Africa toured New Zealand, decisively winning the test series despite losing the first test.

    Controversial Tours

    The 1960 All Blacks tour of South Africa excluded Maori players, leading to a 10-year refusal of tours to the country. The 1976 All Blacks tour of apartheid South Africa resulted in controversy and contributed to the Gleneagles Agreement in 1977. The 1981 South African tour to New Zealand sparked protests against apartheid, and the country experienced unrest during the tour.

    Early World Cups

    New Zealand hosted and won the inaugural World Cup in 1987, beating France 29–9 in the final. By the 1991 World Cup, New Zealand was an aging side, reaching the semi-finals but losing to Australia. In 1995, New Zealand reached the final but lost 12–15 to South Africa in extra time.

    Professionalism

    The professional era in rugby union began in 1995, and New Zealand won the inaugural Tri-Nations in 1996. The All Blacks dominated the 2005 Tri-Nations, achieving a second Grand Slam over the Home Nations. In 2011, New Zealand hosted and won the Rugby World Cup, defeating France 8–7 in the final.

    Hansen Era

    Under coach Steve Hansen, the All Blacks went undefeated in the 2013 Rugby Championship, achieving a 100% record in a calendar year. The 2015 Rugby World Cup saw them retain the title, becoming the first nation to win the tournament three times. The All Blacks continued their success in the 2016 Rugby Championship, going undefeated.

    2019 and Beyond

    In 2019, the All Blacks had a mixed year, suffering a historic loss to Ireland but retaining the Bledisloe Cup. The Rugby World Cup ended in the semi-finals with a loss to England. After the tournament, coach Steve Hansen retired, marking the end of an era for the All Blacks. Ian Foster took over as the new head coach.

    2020-2023 Journey

    From 2020 to 2023, the All Blacks navigated a dynamic journey, overcoming the challenges of the COVID-19 pandemic and securing victories in the Tri-Nations, including the Bledisloe Cup. The subsequent years were marked by dominant performances in 2021, successful defenses of the Bledisloe Cup and Rugby Championship, and a unique ending with back-to-back losses. In 2022, the team faced successes, setbacks, and coaching changes, concluding the season with 8 wins, 4 losses, and 1 draw. The challenges persisted in 2023, with a shortened Rugby Championship and a historic defeat against the Springboks. Despite retaining the Bledisloe and Freedom Cup, they fell short in the Rugby World Cup final. The season closed with a 9-3-0 record, marking the end of the Ian Foster era.

    Robertson Era (Commencing Nov 1, 2023)

    Following the conclusion of the 2023 Rugby World Cup, Scott Robertson assumed the role of head coach from November 1, 2023. His tenure marks a new chapter for the All Blacks, with his first game scheduled for 2024, ushering in a period of transition and anticipation for the team and its supporters.
